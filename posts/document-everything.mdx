> Lists are an inevitable consequence of death - John McCarthy (probably)

As a child, my conception of the “adult world” existed primarily in the form of movies and television, fleeting glances of the media my brother, at seven years my senior, gained access to years before me. The sound of Alias heard just after bedtime, the Tony Montoya poster on the wall of his room, the Neo versus Smith courtyard fight caught askance on his portable DVD player during a road trip. 

My obsession with old media started early, as a result. There was just so much to catch up on. I loved the compilation DVDs of classic shows they used to sell at Cracker Barrel (despite somehow only ever having volumes 2, 3, and 6 of a given show) and constructed my own media studies curriculum out of the discs and tapes that accumulated around the house. And that's how I met Earl.

I can't call to mind any specific memories of coming across My Name is Earl when I was still too young to watch it, but I know it was a show that I saw my parents watching when it aired and that I filed away in that “adult world” category, meaning I just *had* to experience it when I came of age. Like those collections of Twilight Zone DVDs from Cracker Barrel, my folks only ever bought the season 1 box set, but that was more than enough to feel like I had gained entry to the club. 

For the uninitiated, My Name is Earl is a 30-minute sitcom that debuted in 2005 about a ne'er-do-well named Earl Hickey and his attempts to account for his past after winning the lottery. In the pilot, Earl finds a scratch-off winner worth $100,000 but nearly loses it, thereby stumbling into the concept of karma and realizing that he has to use his newfound wealth for the betterment of others lest he lose it again. He makes a big list of everyone he ever wronged and sets out crossing them off.

While I'll never pass up an opportunity to put some shine on a near-forgotten 2000s sitcom starring fellow North Carolina native Jaime Pressly, what I'm really here to talk about today are lists and the people who make them. I've come to realize that the only people who bother making lists are those who feel short on time, as the contemporary (and incredibly saccharine) Morgan Freeman/Jack Nicholson vehicle Bucket List put rather bluntly. [Side note: What was it about the mid 2000s that produced so much list-based content? It's like post-9/11 everyone was scared to be short on time but still hopeful enough to think we could do it all before 2008 quashed that notion.]

But back to the matter at hand: a bucket list is the same thing as a to-do list just on a different time scale. In the show, Earl made a list because he was worried about running out the clock on his good fortune. But the younger version of myself watching? In spite of my desperate need to level the playing field with my older brother and to understand all the pop culture that went over my head as a kid, I never bothered listing them all out because I had all the time in the world to get to them. I *did* make a bucket list shortly after the movie came out, but then again who didn't?

Fast-forward a decade or so and I find myself feeling the urge to make a list. They say there's no one more stressed than a twenty something who thinks they're running out of time, and boy do I believe it. The past year has been full of big changes from my work with Router becoming increasingly federated to getting married. There's nothing quite like the start of a new chapter to put that queasy uncertainty in your belly, and with my wedding firmly in the rear-view and the ole three-oh bearing down on me, it's high time to put up or shut up. 

So I made a list. A real **big** list. *Too* big, even. There are 29 things on it. And I want to do them all before I turn 29 on September 5th. Things I've always wanted to accomplish, things I've always wanted to try, things that might turn out a bit silly, it's all there. Am I liable to get through it all? In all likelihood, no. But I've always been scrappiest with my back against the wall, and I've got this nagging feeling that I've never truly dialed it all the way up to eleven, so I'm going to give it my best shot.

Item number 1 is “Document everything”, and you could argue that I'm crossing it off prematurely because nothing has happened yet, so how could I claim to have documented it all? Simple, really, I've got to introduce the list somehow and it would be pretty anticlimactic to write 28 posts about cool projects and then the grand finale be all “it was the journey not the destination”, so let's get the whole “blogging about blogging” thing out of the way and take this as an IOU for 28 more posts in the next 73 days. Expect them to be littered with references and personal anecdotes and zero LLM oversight. [Oh Lord, were the recipe bloggers right all along?]

To answer the most obvious question, no, I am not going to be previewing the entirety of the list in advance. I do have a full list, but you'll just have to take me at my word. I think part of the fun will be coloring in the details along the way and want to keep what I've got in store a surprise, to a certain extent.

I remain reticent to put together a watchlist. Hell, two out of the three examples I started with up top, Scarface and Alias (excuse me, ALIA<span className="bg-white text-[#03001B]">S</span>), I still haven't gotten around to watching. There's time enough for all that yet. Now, though? Now's the time to *get to work*.
