Welcome back for the second part of this series and for the grand unveiling of the next entry on the list. First, a note on nomenclature: after utilizing the cumbersome phrase “this overarching project” in my personal notes for the umpteenth time, I started referring to it as “The Endeavor” (which for anyone going “wait, isn't that...", I'm free and clear to lay claim on since all the famous ones use the British spelling). If you're picking up the thread here, I introduced The Endeavor in [my previous post](/posts/document-everything). To summarize: it's an overly ambitious list of 29 tasks to accomplish before I turn 29 in September. I'm already behind schedule.

Before the big reveal of the latest project, let me begin by providing a little background (because we all know everyone's favorite part of a blog post is the preamble). Throughout my years working with the [Router](/router) team, we had - at varying times - several visions for what the venture could mature into, from large-scale client agency to tech startup. Right from its inception, I took a lot of inspiration from MSCHF's irreverent approach to the internet and felt there was a lot of fertile ground to explore in a less cynical take on that same sort of nose-thumbing, one that poked fun in an attempt to imagine a better digital world. We had countless ideas for micro-sites in that vein over the years, and some even made their way into client projects, but we could never justify dedicating the margins around those projects to the pursuit because there wasn't a clear path to building an actual business out of the work. 

So today's task - “Design a satirical site” - is overdue in multiple regards. Fortunately, The Endeavor presents an ideal opportunity for this kind of art-for-arts-sake undertaking. Don't get me wrong, there are plenty of plausible business ventures on the list too, but true to form, I'm starting on the non-commercial end of the spectrum. If you really think about it, it's the ideal framework for justifying such ambitions. How else would I get a double dose of accomplishment from something that will, best-case scenario, actively cost me money?

## Introducing URLegy

A lot of my work on the internet has a nostalgic tint to it, and while there's certainly validity to arguments that malign nostalgia as an appeal to trite notions that "things were better in the past", that overlooks a more productive application of the feeling: one that asks *what* it was that was better as a catalyst for action in the here and now. As pertains the old internet, I don't feel the need to join the chorus of voices reminiscing about the era when the web was something that you could surf rather than five corporations in a trench coat. No, productive nostalgia requires *action*. Today, the internet gets the burial it deserves.

[URLegy](https://urlegy.com) is a digital graveyard for dead websites. You can explore its pixel cemetery and leave a flower at the graveside of your dearly departed. Flowers wilt over the course of three days before fading away to nothing. Graves, like memories, require tending, after all. It's worth noting that only websites that are properly dead are interred here. There are many reanimated corpses of the likes of MySpace shambling around these days.

## Design Evolution

URLegy is a classic example of a long-simmering idea, one I've been sitting on for a half year or longer. This next section details the ways my design for the site evolved over time, but it's worth first explaining why the entry on the list didn't just read “Build URLegy” when I obviously had it in mind when I was building out the list. Like I mentioned in my last post, I'm invested in the journey of The Endeavor and want to follow where the road leads, which is why I opted for vaguer phrasing where applicable. Sure, URLegy was the first thing I had in mind when I wrote this list item, but particularly for the later entries, my plans are sure to change. Where the early entries are concerned, however, it helps move things along to have at least a partial notion going in.

When I first conceptualized URLegy, I envisioned it as a memorial site, a la Legacy.com, with obituaries and guestbooks written by users about their favorite defunct websites. The design was meant to take inspiration from the look of such sites in the 2000s and 2010s, and I drafted an initial sketch + site map, as well as a ClipArt style logo.

Besides the obvious technical hurdles of user accounts and moderation, the idea suffered from the same “critical mass” problem as every undergraduate social network concept, namely that URLegy itself would be dead until someone came around and started writing. That was the state of the idea when I picked it back up last week under the guise of The Endeavor. Upon revisiting the concept, I decided to take it in a more “environmental” direction, having grown up in the era of browser games and overworld maps and finding that paradigm sorely lacking in our current day and age. Despite reconceptualizing the experience as a pixel graveyard, I held onto the notion of a guestbook at each grave, reasoning that smaller blurbs would be more easily moderated and less obvious in their absence. That was, until I came upon the more elegant notion of flowers.

The iterative nature of design is how you go from a simple spark of an idea to a quality output, but it is perhaps the part of the process I find most difficult to force. The volume of work inherent in this type of undertaking is designed to force reps at the things I find inspiring but - for whatever reason - don't usually receive priority, with “whatever reason” consisting of approximately 30% the necessity of paid work and 70% the kind of behavior that leads one to write thousands of words on [Hollow Knight](/posts/i-should-go-there-and-do-that). But that same volume is directly at odds with the subconscious processing that I find generates the “a-ha” moments that move the needle on an idea. There's a risk in mythologizing subconscious processing, though, and it's because it's a pretty good excuse for being passive. The fact of that matter remains that there's no replacement for active engagement with an idea and perhaps even a degree of bashing your head against the wall to shake it loose. Epiphany can't form in a vacuum, and I didn't land on URLegy's final form until I reengaged with it.

## A (Working) Theory of Artful AI

What the pixel graveyard lost in technical depth compared to my original concept, it more than made up for in aesthetic requirements. This had the effect of making the overall task considerably more labor intensive since aesthetic demands are precisely the things I refuse to delegate to AI. As someone with an abiding love for the arts, I'm firmly in the camp of “AI art is soulless,” but I've never felt that meant AI can't be used *in service* of art.

I spent half a decade on AI research at UNC, Berkeley, and Stanford, and the majority of that time was focused on AI as an augmentation of human creativity of various forms. They weren't particularly successful projects, and after finding this fact imminently frustrating for a long time, I think it's a lesson that is finally bearing fruit in the agentic age. AI isn't *meant* to be creative. I know firsthand. If you ask an LLM to tell you the most outlandish idea it can come up with, it's going to give you the *most likely* answer to that query given all of its statistical priming, and if the inherent contradiction in that approach isn't apparent, we're probably not going to be friends.

So AI can be in service of art only inasmuch as the most likely answer is desirable. What makes it such a valuable coding tool is precisely the thing that precludes it from the creative act. In my estimation, "Artful AI" is just another name for human-in-the-loop, because the artful part is the human's end of the deal. All that stuff about subconscious processing, the way one's individual experiences uncover surprising connections across disparate domains, is inherently opposed to the notion of a statistical plausibility engine. 

It's a common refrain that with AI, the cost of execution is going to zero, but I think that's a misguided notion. It's not the cost of execution that's going to zero, it's the cost of implementation. Implementation has always been the last step, and when implementation cost goes to zero, everything that precedes it - ideating, designing, producing, architecting, specifying - is still the cost of execution. If the execution cost was zero, I would have been writing this post last week. The advantage of the speed of implementation is that it gives you increased latitude to sweat the small stuff, and if you're not sweating the small stuff, you're not doing art. It's the details that separate creation from fabrication. I always joked that my useful working life would draw to a close if I ever found myself in a managerial role, and here I am, delegating like crazy and busier than ever. And while I empathize with those who found personal meaning in the solving of technical puzzles only to have that replaced with prompt writing and code review, the artfulness of code, as pertains anyone but its maintainers, has only ever surfaced in the presentation layer. And in the presentation layer, details still matter, so art still matters. 

## Tools of the Trade

I thought I'd conclude with a short “making-of” segment on the art and music for URLegy. All the pixel drawings were done by hand in the PixSquare app. Special thanks to my wife, Marissa, for helping with the flowers. It's an alluringly fiddly process to watch a single pixel make all the difference in the world. We got a lot of mileage from experimenting with dithering, long a favorite amongst my Router peers. For a visual introduction to the concept, check out [this excellent guide](https://visualrambling.space/dithering-part-1/).

The music was composed on a Dirtywave M8, nature's answer to the question “what if a calculator could make music?” It's an equally fiddly compositional tool called a tracker. There's a metaphor about detail in there somewhere, but it's left as an exercise for the reader.
