Over what was supposed to be one but has now stretched into the past two weeks, I have been without my primary MacBook while it gets a new battery (and also apparently a fresh set of screws), a long-simmering need for my now 4 year old computer likely hastened by my recent Steam Winter Sale purchase of Baldur's Gate 3. And while I could certainly write an entire post about the ways a simple battery replacement necessitating an entirely new top case and a seven-day turnaround — not to mention an additional week's delay for *screws* — are emblematic of everything flawed with Apple's walled-garden approach to hardware, this is not that post. 

Faced with the prospect of turning back to my Leviathan of a 2015 Intel Mac, I decided to embrace my slightly-less-tethered-but-who-am-I-kidding-the-greatest-tether-is-in-my-pocket-anyways existence save the odd client call or two. Fortunately for my ambition of free soloing life, this laptop limbo happens to coincide with my first truly concerted effort to curb my social media consumption — primarily scrolling Reddit but also occasionally Facebook and Instagram when sufficiently desperate for entertainment. Despite my longstanding distaste for algorithmically mediated content (my Instagram usage never recovered from the day it went non-chronological) and hanging on as long as possible to old Reddit and the 25 post limit, page-breaking, psychological exit ramps afforded by its UI, the slow migration to mobile consumption and its endless scrolls eventually got the better of me. 

I used to be able to justify the time sink by the informational content gleaned, from staying on top of breaking news to feeding my ceaseless appetite for useless trivia, but the diminishing returns on time invested have become increasingly difficult to ignore. As with many things, It would be easy to straw-man AI as the proximate cause — and I definitely think it's a significant factor — but it's perhaps even more likely that Reddit has just hit its own peak [enshittification](https://pluralistic.net/2023/01/21/potemkin-ai/#hey-guys) phase. We already experienced the migration of value from users to businesses customers in the form of moderation policy changes both long-overdue and seemingly arbitrary, and as those changes mature into an oft-toxic moderator culture and the ultimate clawing back of value from all parties involved in service of shareholders, we've arrived at end-stage platform decay.

## Enter the Knight

All to say I had very high-minded ambitions about what I would accomplish with my freshly liberated time — chief among them writing and composing — but like any proficient distraction seeker I found a shiny new substitute for my dopamine deficit: Hollow Knight. I've long owned a copy for Switch (and almost mistakenly bought a second for my MacBook in the aforementioned Steam Sale), longer than I have even been aware of the Metroidvania genre to which it belongs. My limited experience to that point consisted of the first level or so of Metroid Prime 3, a lovingly ill-informed childhood purchase from the NYC Nintendo Store, and a limited yet fascinating foray with one of the Castlevania games via the Wii Virtual Console. 

Combined with the lower tolerance for difficulty I had before becoming hopelessly enamored with Soulslikes, I don't think my initial attempt at the game made it much further than Fog Canyon or the Fungal Wastes — in other words, not very far at all. I'm pretty certain I at least beat Hornet and got the dash ability, so it's not like I was completely unaware of the progressive unlocking of mechanics at the core, it was more a sense of frustration and disorientation at the lack of guidelines and impenetrability of its world and how that flew in the face of the conventions of the games I was heretofore acquainted with. It goes to show my lack of awareness of the extent to which that was the point, as well as my inability to appreciate what the game was trying to achieve in terms of difficulty, exploration, and environmental storytelling.

With the long-awaited arrival of its sequel, Silksong, Hollow Knight has very much been back in the public consciousness (and never left for a good portion of its extremely devoted fanbase). As a proud new Switch 2 owner who bet that RAM shortages would accomplish what tariffs could not — a price hike — I started a fresh save armed with the wisdom garnered over a full run of Metroid Dread (as well as an excellent indie entry to the genre, [GRIME](https://store.steampowered.com/app/1123050/GRIME/)) and a hard fought appetite for punishment forged in the 10 FPS bowels of Blighttown on an OG Xbox 360. I felt that I was well poised for a newfound appreciation of the game's artistry. Appreciate it I did.

Over the 60-some hours I have now devoted to getting to 111% completion in-game — one percent shy of 100% (chew on that math nerds) — and certain that the Pantheon of the Knight will yield to me sooner rather than later, I feel like I've arrived at a theory of what makes well-executed non-linear games so compelling: a cascading, implicit series of “I should go there and do that” prompts. In some regards, it may seem like an obvious statement at first, but it holds a lot of explanatory weight that ties together Hollow Knight, open-world games like Elden Ring and Breath of the Wild (and my first love, Skyrim, endless porting and memes notwithstanding), and even such far flung examples as Stardew Valley.

## Welcome to Hallownest

As you explore the myriad environments of Hollow Knight's setting, Hallownest, the new abilities you acquire fall into two rough categories: combat enhancements like stronger attacks or new moves and traversal upgrades like a double jump. Generally speaking, the combat enhancements serve the traditional purpose of making the player feel stronger over time, equipping you for progressively stronger enemies while increasingly trivializing the old foes you are guaranteed to reencounter in such a backtrack-centric game. The possibilities invoked by new traversal options, on the other hand, are what really set the gears turning. 

A large portion of what makes Hollow Knight so compelling, in my experience, is its ability to present a frontier that feels like its almost always expanding; there are vanishingly few moments where the possibilities seem to contract. The internal experience of playing the game is a series of branching decision points that multiply, drop away, and loop back on themselves in a manner that consistently produce at least a handful of headings to explore or re-investigate. The fact that your mental to-do list never dwindles makes it a challenge to put the game down — an irresistible magnetism drawing you further in. There's always a new corner to look around or a old fork to revisit.

Hollow Knight's greatest feat is how it manages to build a world that is enigmatic without ever becoming indecipherable. The experience of reaching a new area or discovering a secret would not feel nearly as satisfying if the game outright told you where to go and what to do, but players need *some* indication of what's required of them or else they're at risk of feeling alienated. My first experiences with truly difficult games (i.e. those made by FromSoftware) were unabashedly guide-heavy, but the more I played, the more I came to appreciate what games can achieve when they tell you less. I only ever felt compelled to reach for a guide at the tail end of my Hollow Knight experience because of how expertly Team Cherry weaves breadcrumbs throughout, with two obvious examples coming to mind related to the Grubs, a hidden collectible/quasi-trophy system in the game. The first is the way sound is of consequence in the many corners of Hallownest (beyond the kind of excellent soundtrack that makes hard bosses worth the attempts required): Grubs make a distinct, localized sound that clues you in when you get near one that there is a secret to uncover, even if you can't see it. Despite such hints, I still assumed I would eventually need help tracking down the last few given there was no sure fire way to even know which ones I had missed, *until* the game provides you with an item that provides precisely that information, a moment of peak game design that I know I would not have appreciated in the same way had I already been relying on external assistance.

## Differing Means with Similar Ends

What Hollow Knight achieves with a densely knotted puzzle box of a world, two of the other exemplars I referenced accomplish through visual spectacle: Elden Ring and, to a slightly lesser extent, Breath of the Wild. Elden Ring is arguably one of the purest exploratory gaming experiences ever created. It's minimalistic UI approach, embedded quest design, and enchanting vistas leave you picking a landmark on the horizon and uncovering every ruin, crypt, and cave along the way. Perhaps the most sublime experience I've ever had in gaming is watching the scale of the world map progressively expand over the early game to hint at areas that are quite literally dozens of hours away. There are exceedingly few requirements for a given playthrough, instead a series of invitations. Breath of the Wild engenders a lot of similar moments to Elden Ring — encouraging detours for shrines and other interesting waypoints as you traverse the map — but opts to make certain aspects, like quests, more explicit. An interesting juxtaposition emerges in the challenges presented as you explore: Elden Ring's enemy mobs are its greatest and most notorious test, while Breath of the Wild often turns the environment itself into a gauntlet to overcome. To be fair, Elden Ring presents its fair share of environmental hazards, but in true FromSoftware fashion, they are more in the vein of cruel pranks on the player as opposed to the beguiling puzzles of Hyrule's landscape.

The proliferation of open world tropes over the last decade or so of gaming history is a tacit acknowledgment that this type of self-reinforcing flywheel of goal formation and achievement is one of the most compelling things a game can do, and an open world is a helpful, though not requisite, tool for building it. When executed poorly, however, open-world games devolve into linear games with extra set dressing, commonly observed into the all-too-familiar fetch quest sending you to the far reaches of a world map solely for the excuse of that area existing at all. *something something* it insists upon itself *something something*.

The last game I want to discuss, Stardew Valley, might initially appear a bit further afield, but its uniqueness serves to show the variety of mechanics that can impel deep engagement. While ostensibly a farming game, the game also presents such options as fishing, relationship management, and even clearing caves filled with monsters. With so many possibilities in a given day mediated by a day/night cycle that enforces a strict time limit, Stardew evokes the “just one more” feeling as proficiently as all-time powerhouses like Civilization and classic Call of Duty because there's always something you want to do the next time the sun rises, whether thats harvesting long-awaited strawberries, a special event on the calendar, or maybe just hearing that banger of a soundtrack again.

## Leaving Room for Play

What sets exceptional non-linear games above both their non-linear peers and traditional linear experiences are the implicit desires they create to participate in the world. Linear games get their sense of purpose by default. When you press start in Super Mario, there's not much ambiguity in your directive: go right. They are fundamentally prescriptive, both in the sense of a particular sequence of actions being required and, often, being explicitly told as much. You *must* go there and do this. But what they gain in clarity they lose out on in agency. If going “off script” equals a one way ticket to a game over screen, players can never be empowered to construct the system of personal prioritization that drives “I should go there and do that” euphoria. Non-linear games are replete with design challenges of their own, of course, not least of which is the inherent tension is conveying narrative without undermining player choice (the alternative being to leave all semblance of meaning-construction to the players themselves). The demand for some vague notion of sequence even in well-executed games tends to rear its head in the form of an explicit checklist at one juncture or another — Dreamers in Hollow Knight, Shardbearers in Elden Ring, and Divine Beasts in Breath of the Wild as the relevant examples. Stardew doesn't have a direct corollary, but its also the example with the loosest narrative.

A curious side effect I've noticed in some, but not all, of these games, is a cliff where a certain completionist impulse takes hold. I've never been a major achievement-hunter in games, neither of the high score nor trophy variety. Linear fare just tends not to warrant enough sustained interest to fret over the minutiae, but both Elden Ring and now Hollow Knight have gotten me to turn over near every rock. In the case of Breath of the Wild and Stardew Valley, the sheer amount of content at offer outlasted my personal sense of purpose, though that's not to say that is inherently worse by comparison or that they lacked a feeling of conclusion (which tends to, smartly, come in stages in such games). 

It's worth noting that out of the four games at mention, two also happen to be some of the most successful indie games of all time. First, it goes to show that “I should go there and do that” is not a tool restricted to the sole purview of the big studios (those tend to be mostly powered by elbow grease as opposed to clever design). As a designer-developer with a personal interest in games, I think its also a valuable lesson in the types of things we should keep in mind when sculpting mechanics, environments, and progression, and the broader experiences that can emerge when these individual pieces are approached with intentionality.
