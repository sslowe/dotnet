It's full steam ahead on the Endeavor this week. You can catch back up on the journey from the start [here](/posts/document-everything). In short, I'm working my way through a list of 29 things I'd like to accomplish before my 29th birthday in September. Yes, I know this is \#4. No, I don't have enough time, thank you for asking.

## Thinking! Fun with friends?

An interesting side effect that I'm beginning to notice of overly ambitious list-making is that big lists tend to beget many, smaller lists: the obvious ones (like weekly and daily todos), the exploratory enumerations (mainly possibilities for tasks or areas of research), and the odd reflective exercise or two. It is in this last vein that I've been slowly building out a list entitled “Things I Like to Do.” The first thing I added, and which I have ample opportunity for in this Endeavor, is creative brainstorming. One of my favorite parts of any project has always been the process of refining an initial spark of inspiration into an executable vision, replete with all the unexpected ways it can evolve on the way there, and I have 29 opportunities to do exactly that.

The trickiest balancing act to strike when attempting to develop a concept lies between actively engaging with it and letting things cook up into a nice, subconscious stew, something [I discussed previously](/posts/design-a-satirical-site) as it related to my design process for [URLegy](https://urlegy.com). In the absence of external pressure, it's easy for a new idea to come into existence as a “hey, wouldn't that be interesting?” and promptly stop dead in its tracks. Part of my aim in inflicting the framework of the Endeavor upon myself is precisely to generate that kind of pressure and see a whole litany of concepts through to the finish line. There's something to be said, however, for the act of ideating in a group setting and the crucible that is multiple stakeholders on a project, particularly close collaborators whose taste you trust. In my case, that's one of the great joys of working with my partners at [Router](/router) and our musician clientele. It's a niche that is incredibly amenable to outlandish notions, after all. In fact, the refrain most often heard at the outset of any engagement has always been “I'm not sure if this is possible, but..." 

Between the solo nature of this Endeavor and the general evolution of my work towards something more akin to freelancing, I find myself “brainstorming for one” more often than not. Writing, as always, proves an exceptional tool for crystallizing thought, either in the form of these posts or my own, personal notes, and while I'm sure you can tell how fascinating it is inside my own skull, my past experience dictates that even the best ideas benefit when they extend beyond the individual. It's in that spirit that I included on the list “Apply to South Park Commons” or SPC, for short.

## No, not that South Park

I have *complicated* feelings about venture capital. On one hand, I attended undergrad in the heyday of subsidized user acquisition (and I have the receipts for all the sub-$10 Ubers I took around Chapel Hill as a result). On the other, I have reservations around the way capital shifts the incentive structure for businesses. My philosophy is and always will be that you should strive to create the best, most useful thing possible, but that kind of efficiency is not always the most profitable thing. <span className="text-[#77FFE4] text-sm">[I'm looking at you, Google Search.]</span>

That being said, many of the lulls in Router's client work were filled with incubator applications, and out of all our attempts, the one interview we were ever granted was for SPC's Founder Fellowship. We submitted an artful fitness app concept called Source, and like the majority of our ideas, I'm not sure if it was really all that investable. But therein lies the difference that makes SPC the “anti-incubator.” Whereas an incubator like YCombinator is measuring the fit between your business and their model, at SPC the individual is the model. As far as they are concerned, finding the right person is the most important goal, whether that be a former founder or an Olympian. So I've decided to throw my hat into the ring once more, this time for their Member Residency instead of the Founder Fellowship, and find out if I'm one of those right people. More than anything, I think I would greatly enjoy the opportunity to immerse myself in a community of engaged individuals with a wide array of interests, “wide array” of course being a euphemism for any number of LLMs. I kid, I kid. Mostly, anyways.

There's nothing quite like an application to stir something primal within those who have, at one time or another, been labeled “high-achieving,” and by primal I mean that feeling you got from the first family member or teacher who made the mistake of praising you *just a little* too much. Ask any of my fellow Morehead-Cain Scholars if you don't believe me. Many find themselves disappointed at the dearth of pedestals presented in adult life to be placed upon. That's why we count so many doctors amongst our ranks. The calling is beside the point. It's all the **applications** you get to fill out. Still others have to get their affirmation fixes in other, less stomachable ways. *Cough* consulting *cough*

My ~~mother~~ editor says rejection is good for the soul. Plus, now I get to cross it off the list, and we all know I need every opportunity to shorten that list that I can get.
