Thanks for joining me for another entry in my grand Endeavor to cram a growing volume of work in a steadily waning number of weeks before I turn 29. Catch back up on the inadequate number of tasks in the rearview [starting here](/posts/document-everything).

This time around, I have gone and done something that I can scarcely afford to do. There are enough convoluted items on the list that the comparatively straightforward ones are meant to be precisely that. So when I included this task, “Share a poem,” I intended it to be a fun, short exercise to harken back to the poetry courses I enjoyed so much at UNC. Maybe submit it to a journal or the like just for the sake of it. You know, easy.

Instead, I built a whole site.

## Programmers have feelings, too

The curious part of where I landed with this one is not how exactly I got so derailed. No, that's par for the course. Rather, it's that in aiming to recapture the spirit of my forays into the English department, I wound up right back in the COMP classes I know oh so well. [I've written before](/posts/what-i-learned-in-slorking-school-is) about the instant affinity I felt for computer science my freshman year, and by the time I made it to the elective portion of the degree, I had a whole laundry list of courses I wanted to take, like COMP 426: Modern Web Programming. <span className="text-[#77FFE4] text-sm">[There was a time when I resented being called a nerd. The fact that there were always more classes I wanted to take than credits I had to fill is evidence I never had much standing to begin with.]</span>

It is with only the slightest trepidation that I share now my final project from COMP 426 (and the oldest piece of publicly available software I helped co-author): [Devetry](https://github.com/comp426-dead-poets-society/comp426-devetry). Fun fact, I still get the Depend-a-Bot alerts 7 years after the fact. At last count, there were 303 open vulnerabilities. One can only assume that any attempts to run the program would result in the immediate self-destruction of your machine. 

As with the vast majority of my academic projects, Devetry was an attempt to bridge my passions in technology and the arts. The site consisted of a feed of posts in the form of [p5.js](https://p5js.org/) canvases, with the idea that each one would contain a poem alongside a visually interactive element corresponding to the text. Developer poetry. Devetry. Get it?

Today, I am proud to say that Devetry lives on. I came up with a better name this time around, though.

## Code of Lines

[*Code of Lines*](https://codeoflines.com) is a direct successor to Devetry and yet another experiment around the theme of [artful collaboration with AI](/posts/design-a-satirical-site). The advantage over the original (besides seven years of better taste and further experience) is that the tools no longer demand you be the expert in all things. The posts on Devetry were limited by my understanding of p5, cribbed together from their well-curated references (and StackOverflow, RIP). Now, the only constraining factor is a clear vision and a poem worth sharing, not that that was ever the easy part. 

The poem I have written, [“Telescope”](https://codeoflines.com/telescope), took shape over the course of a long subway ride to the dentist. I've always found the subway to be a place conducive to deep thought. The only problem is that this time I wound up so deep in thought that I got on a train headed in the wrong direction. Of course when I had an appointment.

I've developed what might seem to be a strange association between the MTA and verse because of their [Poetry in Motion](https://www.mta.info/agency/arts-design/poetry-in-motion) collaboration with the Poetry Society of America. I figured that I had read enough poetry on the train ([Uncertainty Principle at Dawn](https://www.mta.info/agency/arts-design/collection/uncertainty-poem) is my personal favorite), that it was about time to spend a ride or two writing some of my own. <span className="text-[#77FFE4] text-sm">[I'm sure the sight of me frantically tapping out syllables on my thigh did not even chart in anyone's top 5 manic subway moments of the day.]</span>

It was somewhere in the midst of the composition process that I realized I was not going to be taking the simple route of writing a nice post about my love of poetry and slapping the poem somewhere in the body. There was such a clear visual accompanying the text in my mind that it led me straight back to the idea I had all those years prior with Devetry.

*Code of Lines* is home to only the one poem for now, and considering the litany of tasks I have in front of me, it might be a while before I have another chance to expand it. I just can't resist a branding exercise and a top-tier domain name. In an ideal world, the site could be home to the work of any number of poet-grammers, so I'm leaving the door open for submissions. See the site for more details!
